F1 FOR BEGINNERS

New to Formula 1? Start here. Everything you need to know to join the fastest sport on earth.

Formula 1 is the pinnacle of open-wheel racing — the fastest, most technologically advanced, and most-watched motorsport series on the planet. Twenty drivers, ten teams, and over 20 races across five continents every season. It's a blend of engineering brilliance, athletic endurance, and strategic genius.

A typical Grand Prix covers approximately 305 km (190 miles) and lasts around 1.5 to 2 hours. Races are limited to a maximum of 2 hours. Some circuits have fewer laps (like Monaco at 78 laps) while others have more (like Monza at 53 laps), but the total distance stays roughly the same.

Max Verstappen (Red Bull) is the reigning champion and dominant force. Lewis Hamilton (Ferrari) holds the record for most wins ever. Charles Leclerc and Lando Norris are the rising challengers gunning for the crown. The grid is stacked with talent — any race weekend can produce a surprise.

F1 combines extreme speed, cutting-edge technology, dramatic rivalries, and global glamour like no other sport. The Netflix series "Drive to Survive" brought millions of new fans, but the real hook is the unpredictability — rain, crashes, pit stop failures, strategy gambles. Every race is a mini-movie.

Qualifying takes place on Saturday before the Sunday race. Drivers compete to set the fastest single lap time. It's a three-round knockout: Q1 eliminates the slowest 5, Q2 eliminates the next 5, and Q3 is a shootout among the top 10 for pole position. The fastest driver starts at the front of the grid.

DRS (Drag Reduction System) opens a flap on the rear wing to reduce drag and boost straight-line speed by ~15 km/h — but only when a driver is within 1 second of the car ahead in designated zones. A pit stop is when a car pulls into the pit lane to change tires, make repairs, or adjust strategy. The fastest pit stops take under 2 seconds to change all four wheels.
